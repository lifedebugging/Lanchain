In the beginning, there was nothing. Then there was something. Scientists and
philosophers have spent millennia debating the details of that particular
transition, and in doing so have produced a truly impressive quantity of text,
most of which is not included here for reasons of space and editorial discretion.

What we do know, with reasonable confidence, is that approximately 13.8 billion
years ago, a singularity expanded in what is now known as the Big Bang. From
that moment, matter and energy began their long, complicated relationship — a
relationship that would eventually produce hydrogen clouds, then stars, then
heavier elements, then planets, then oceans, then single-celled organisms, then
multicellular life, then vertebrates, then mammals, then primates, then humans,
and then, inevitably, the internet.

The universe did not plan for the internet. But here we all are.

Mountains were raised and seas were filled. Continents drifted like slow,
enormous rafts upon the mantle of the Earth. Ice ages came and went. Dinosaurs
flourished magnificently for 165 million years before being rudely interrupted
by an asteroid with very poor timing. Mammals, seizing the opportunity, diversified
into every available ecological niche with the enthusiasm of a startup entering
an untapped market.

Somewhere in Africa, a lineage of great apes discovered that walking upright
freed up the hands for other activities — carrying things, making tools, gesturing
dramatically during arguments. Over hundreds of thousands of years, brains grew
larger. Fire was tamed. Language emerged. And with language came stories, and
with stories came culture, and with culture came the desire to write things down.

And here we are.
